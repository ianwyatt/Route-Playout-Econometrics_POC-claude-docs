# 116.02 - Board Briefing

**Date:** 10 March 2026

---

## What This Is

Route's audience model provides average audiences at each frame. The mobile volume index adds a layer of temporal variation — using O2 mobile device data to capture hourly footfall fluctuations at 16,265 frame locations that Route's averages don't reflect.

Across 833 real campaigns: **mobile-indexed audiences are 5% higher than Route's estimate. 73% of campaigns gain.** The difference is concentrated at transport hubs, event venues, and high-footfall urban centres — locations where real-world footfall varies most from the average.

---

## The Numbers

44% of campaigns gain more than 5%. Only 7% lose more than 5%. The 27% that see a decrease have frames in quieter suburban or off-peak locations where real footfall is lower than modelled — the decreases are small (mean −3.2%).

## The Evidence

We cross-referenced mobile index spikes against real-world events across 22 stadiums and venues over 13 investigations.

**85% of the top 20 spike dates within the playout window matched confirmed events** — Premier League GW1–7, Champions League nights, Notting Hill Carnival, Reading Festival, Billy Joel at Cardiff.

- Football match days produce **3–11x spikes** at frames near grounds
- A frame near Sunderland's Stadium of Light hit **35x** — confirmed as a Bruce Springsteen concert
- **Saturday accounts for 37%** of extreme spikes, Sunday 26%, peaking 19:00–22:00
- 58% of extreme spikes occur away from stadiums — **transport hubs, high streets, retail**

The geographic hotspot map reads like a list of Premier League grounds and major venues: Manchester, Cardiff, Stretford, Holloway (Emirates), Tottenham, Liverpool, Brentford, Sunderland.

**It's not just football.** The playout window (Aug–Oct) coincides with football season, so the campaign data is dominated by match-day spikes. But the broader O2 dataset shows the index responding to concerts (Springsteen 35x, Take That 14.5x), music festivals (TRNSMT 18.6x, Lytham 8.9x), and horse racing (Royal Ascot 11x). These events fall outside the current playout data range — with contemporaneous mobile and playout data, campaigns near these venues would show the same kind of measurable impact variation.

---

## What to Be Aware Of

**The index measures deviation from what's typical at each location.** A Saturday match at a ground that hosts matches most Saturdays already has that footfall in its Saturday baseline — so individual matches show modest deviation. A one-off concert at Sunderland shows 35x because that's unusual for the location.

**This depends on Route's base audiences.** Where Route already reflects regular event footfall, the index correctly shows little deviation. If Route's base doesn't fully capture it, both would understate the true audience — the index measures deviation from a baseline, it can't correct it.

**Stadium data gap.** We assessed Wembley, Emirates, Stamford Bridge, and Etihad — none have mobile index data in their immediate Output Areas. Where adjacent OA data exists, fan spillover is captured (Emirates 70% match-day uplift, Stamford Bridge 38%).

**POC scope.** This POC uses digital playout data only — 16,265 digital frames matched to O2 mobile index data. Classic (paper poster) frames are excluded because playout data currently only covers digital — there is no timestamped record of when a paper poster is displayed. Industry plans exist to bring classic poster scheduling into playout systems. The O2 data is from 2024, mapped onto 2025 playouts with day-of-week preserved.

## Questions

**Does this make some campaigns look worse?**
Yes — 27% see a decrease. That's the point. It's measuring real footfall, not applying a flat uplift. Only 7% lose more than 5%.

**Is the +5% just inflation?**
No. The index averages exactly 1.0 across all frame-hours. The net positive comes from high-footfall periods coinciding with high audiences — it's a measurement difference, not an artificial boost.

**Why don't major stadiums show the biggest spikes?**
Two reasons. First, there's a data gap — the major stadiums (Wembley, Emirates, Stamford Bridge, Etihad) don't have OOH frames with mobile index data in their immediate Output Areas, though adjacent OAs capture fan spillover (Emirates 70%, Stamford Bridge 38%). Second, venues that host events every Saturday already have those events baked into their baseline — a match at a ground that hosts matches most Saturdays is typical, so the index correctly shows modest deviation. The biggest spikes come from one-off events at locations where that level of footfall is unusual.
